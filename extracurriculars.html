Photography

Photography was a B Term Extracurricular run by Mr. Ellis at MAMS. I always had an interest in photography and had DSLR at home, but I never had any motivation to take pictures. This club completely changed my previous views, and when the weather gets warmer (it's too cold to go outside 😄) I can't wait to take more pictures!

In the club, every week on Tuesdays we would walk to surrounding areas and take pictures of buildings, nature, leaves, ponds, trees, and various other subjects with photographic principles in mind.

At first, I was frustrated that my pictures were either blurry, out of focus, saturated, etc... But after a couple of walks, I started to realize that patience is required to take a good picture. You might have to take twenty shots of the same subject but if it results in a good picture, then it's worth it.

Cyberpatriot

Cyberpatriot is a national youth cyber education program that conducts yearly competitions to help direct students toward careers in cybersecurity. The club is run by Mrs. Taricco and it has a mix of juniors and seniors.

The competitions are hackathon-style in which you are given a six-hour time window to complete 5 assignments. The first three are fixing security vulnerabilities in Windows, Windows Server, and Ubuntu. The fourth assignment is to complete a short Cisco networking quiz. And the last assignment is something called packet tracer which is a network simulator and you are tasked with setting up a network by completing a series of steps.

You can compete with 5 other people (mine only had 4) and my team consisted of Sashank Tadimeti, Rajat Baldawa, Svabhu Govindaraj and Stephen Cooley. Our team made it to the highest division, platinum, and we did fairly well in the state overall. Moreover, we had a lot of fun as a bunch of CS nerds and got a lot of free pizza and candy. Mock Trial

At my sending school, I was on the Mock Trial team for 2 years. Mock Trial is an extracurricular where you simulate a courtroom trial. You can either be an attorney or witness and the goal is to convince the judge to give a verdict in your favor. The two “teams” are the plaintiff and the defense. The trial is based on a fictional case that is compiled all into a case packet. The case packet is comprised of both general laws and affidavits, or testimonies, from 6 different witnesses. These are the only materials you get, and teams are expected to read through them thoroughly to compete in court.

This picture was taken at Clark University for our Round of 32 trial. We represented the defense in this trial.

At our school, we had a fall and winter/spring season, so it was almost a yearlong extracurricular. In the fall we would compete against 8 of the best teams in Massachusetts in the Garden City Invitational as practice for the Massachusetts Mock Trial Tournament. In November, the case packet from the Mass Bar would be released and throughout January and February, we would compete in 3 trials against other teams. If you win all 3 trials, your team competes in a single-elimination tournament where a winner is finally selected. Our team met for around 7 hours a week, so it was a bit of a time commitment. When we weren't working, we would order pizza or just gossip about things going on in school (I was one of the few guys on the team) which made the meetings more fun.

This was our fall team at the Garden City Invitational Tournament at Newton South. You can see the attorney table has a mess of examinations, affidavits, and case laws. Our captain, on the right, is using sticky notes to silently communicate with the attorneys (and also talk smack about the other team ツ).

All in all, I found Mock Trial very interesting because of its variability and complexity. The sport is similar to speech and debate because you have to make a solid argument, but it’s also similar to theater because you have to rehearse your lines and be able to “perform” in court. I served as the team’s expert witness which was an added level of difficulty because I had to offer an opinion about the case as a professional in a certain field. This meant that one year, I had to learn about audio engineering and the next, I learnt about computer vision in autonomous vehicles. Even though I am not participating in this sport this year, I will never forget all the people I met who helped me discover my potential.

To all students applying to Mass Academy, don’t join an extracurricular just to add it to your resume. Do it because you truly enjoy it. As a STEM-minded student, I actually wanted to quit mock trial in the first week thinking it was totally useless for college applications. But after I got to know it more, I enjoyed it and learnt more from the sport than I ever imagined I would.

If you have made it all the way here and you’re interested in Mock Trial, you can learn more about the sport at www.mocktrialstrategies.com. This website helped me a lot with writing examinations and general tips on performance.

CAD

In ninth grade, I decided to take up CAD for my technology requirement. At first, I thought it was just some dumb elective, but as I worked more with it and got to know the teacher, I loved the field and its prospects.

Well, what is CAD? CAD, or a Computer Aided Design, is a method to drafting parts on a computer. Essentially, you get to design 3d models and can enter in measurements for it. The cool part about is that you can change any of the measurements to scale the model, or just shift it to your needs.

Through that elective, I got to design many different parts and assemblies. Some were industrial, but some were just fun and creative to do.

I loved CAD so much, that I enrolled in the CAD program at MAMS for A Term. Even though the software is different (I used to Creo Parametric), I am excited to create new parts and 3d print new projects.
